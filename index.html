UNDERSTANDING YOUR CAMERA

Camera types (briefly)

  • Phone camera
  • Point-and-shoot
    • "pocket"
    • "point and shoot"
    • "advanced"
  • SLR (Single Lens Reflex)
  • Medium-format

As you go up the scale, you get better "auto", more control, and new capabilities (i.e. physics)

P&S vs. SLR

Lens vs. Camera

  • The lens controls the physics (the light)*
  • The camera controls the lens
  • So the lens has physical limits
  • And the camera has tools for working within those limits

* Also see 'film', later

Focus

Why you should care about it:

  • Bad focus == bad picture

Understanding focus:

  • Moves lenses back and forth w.r.t. each other

Managing focus:

  • Let your camera do this for you, 90% of the time
  • But use your half-click to be sure it knows what you want!

Equipment details (focus):

  • Newer cameras have face-sensors basically to do the half-click for you
  • SLRs and some P&S will have selectable focus reticles
  • SLRs can let you focus manually
    • But... don't
  • Shutter lag
  • Spend more money to get: faster focus, low-light improvements, better guess-the-subject

Shutter speed

Why you should care about it:

  • To avoid blurry pictures!
    • Camera shake
    • Subject motion

Understanding shutter speed:

  • How long the film is exposed to light
  • Longer == more light == brighter image

Managing shutter speed:

  • Faster shutter speed (i.e. shorter exposure) compensates for shake and motion
    • ...but makes the picture darker (all else being equal)
  • Treat 1/60 as a minumum

Equipment details (shutter speed):

  • Your phone (probably) doesn't let you control this
  • All other types let you control it, if you want
  • The half-click will do its best to guess what you want
  • Scene modes prioritize "minimize blur" or "maximize brightness"
  • Spend more $$$ to get: more control, faster max speed, slower min speed ("bulb"), less vibration

Focal length ("zoom")

Why you should care about it:

  • Have a strong subject!
  • See the whole scene!
  • Adjust your physical distance
  • Depth of field! (See future slide.)

Let's also make an example or two

Understanding it (zoom):

  • Humans call this "zoom" but photographers call it "focal length"
    • The former describes what happens to the picture, the latter describes what happens in the camera
  • Zooming in is dangerous, because it (usually) lets less light through
    • (example of a tube)

Managing it (zoom):

  • Zoom in as far as you can... and then maybe a little more
    • But watch out for shutter speed!
  • More zoom is generally better for portraits

Equipment details (zoom):

  • Your phone has no zoom - just crop ("digital zoom")
    • Pretty much every other (modern) camera has optical zoom
  • Zoom has downsides (beyond "less light"):
    • Barrel distortion
    • Sharpness
  • Rule of thumb: 1:1 between shutter speed and focal length (to avoid blur)
  • Spend more money to get: less of all the downsides (more light is most expensive), wider, longer, more range

Aperture

Why you should care about it:

  • Depth of field   ← The holy grail!

Understanding it (aperture):

  • "How big is the opening"
  • Bigger == brighter == narrower depth of field
    • (and vice-versa)
  • Numbering scheme ("F-stop") designed by engineers, not users
    • Smaller number == bigger opening
  • But if you just think of the number as indicating the depth of field...
    • f1.4 == tight!
    • f32 == forever!

Pro photographers everywhere want to kill me now

Managing it (depth of field):

  • For "family" photography you usually want it as tight as possible
  • Unless there's a mountain or the Statue of Liberty in the background
  • So you need to manage it!

Also, I lied (by omission):

  • Zoom also affects depth of field, just about as much as aperture
  • But zoom is usually determind by the subject
    • Or you move your feet, which undoes the change in depth of field
  • And often zoom forces you into a different aperture
  • So in "family" photography, it's reasonable to just equate aperture to depth of field

Equipment details (depth of field):

  • Your phone has no way to limit depth of field
    • Nearly everything is in focus, all the time
  • P&S cameras are a little better, but really you need an SLR to have tight depth of field
  • Be careful of the aperture extremes - they affect sharpness and color, especially at the edges of the image
  • Spend more to get: tighter depth of field
    • (er, bigger aperture)

NOW TO TAKE EVEN BETTER PICTURES

First step:

  • Have
  • a lens
  • and camera
  • that are capable of what you need.

Second step:

Be comfortable with them.

Third step:

Make a picture, don't take one.

To make a picture, part 1:

  • Choose your subject
  • Choose your physical location
    • Pay attention to imposition, angle, lighting, eyes, background, edges
  • Choose your zoom

You do these things already, but maybe not consciously

To make a picture, part 2:

  • Choose your depth of field (aperture)
  • Use your half-click (focus and shutter speed)
  • Check your shutter speed
    • If it's not fast enough (for your zoom and subject motion), change something
      • Zoom is easiest/fastest to change
      • Or stabilize the camera
      • Or use flash
      • Or live with the slow shutter speed
      • Or tell your camera to make a darker picture

Then sieze the moment!
